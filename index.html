Montauk — where Long Island runs out of land

A four-century fishing town that became a moorland resort, a wartime fortress, and a bohemian hideaway — all on one windswept point. Tap through its story, then build your day.

How the point came to be

A town built in chapters

Montauk wasn't planned so much as layered — by the Montaukett who named it, a lighthouse George Washington signed off on, a railroad baron's failed port, a developer's gilded dream, and a war. Tap a year.

25 ways to spend the day

Explore the point

Filter by what you're in the mood for. Everything here is a real local favorite — from the oldest lighthouse in the state to the clam shack you'll pass on the way in.

What locals protect

Montauk fought to keep its salt while the Hamptons crept east. The real one is fishermen at dawn and surfers at dusk.

The mom-and-pops

Most of Main Street is still small, family-run shops — not chains. Locals guard that fiercely.

Fish off the boat

Home to New York's largest fishing fleet. You can buy what was landed that morning, right at the docks.

The off-season

~4,000 year-round residents; ~30,000 in summer. Many love October best — empty beaches, seals on the rocks.

Sunset as ritual

The Montauket dive bar over Fort Pond Bay is the unofficial town gathering. Get there early.
